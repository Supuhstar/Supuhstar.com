The river's mist has long since drenched your robes.

Hours of sitting on this old root will do that, listening to the forest and reaching out. You know what it's telling you, it's the same as the past thirty times you've done this.

You open your eyes.

The canyon is always more beautiful than it seems it should be. The way you feel energies flow through it, it's almost as if the rock itself is alive.

You slowly stand yourself up, stretching and shaking out the stiffness that comes from this annual ritual.

<!-- You've never seen it since that day. It's become just as much a legend in your mind as it is on the lips of the elders who tell its stories and sing its songs.-->

The long shadows thank you as you begin the pilgrimage home. The forest seems to always make sure you know its appreciation for your dedication to it, even if that dedication feels fruitless back home.

Coexistence feels so easy, so natural. You give to the forest, hear its needs, and only take what it can give back... and you're more than happy. It never made sense to you how so many of your neighbors can bear the burden of fighting this, taking little bits and pieces of it and raising those all on their own as if they can be separated from the whole. As if they can be separated from the whole.

---

The familiar warm hug of your spouce shakes you from your thoughts.

"I was worried you might miss the feast!" she says, her ever-supportive eyes reflecting her relief and comfort seeing you return.

Once you've walked arm-in-arm through the drier brush at the edge of the forest, the solstice feast greets you. The familiar mixed aroma of roasted meats, simmering stews, and freshly-baked breads mingles with woodsmoke and enthusiastic music. Children dart between the legs of their elders, laughter as bright against the night as the bonfire. Your spouse gives your hand a final squeeze before releasing it, her smile warm as she moves to help with the last preparations.

You make your way through the gathering, acknowledging nods and greetings with quiet grace. The familiar faces and voices of your community surround you, but your mind still lingers in the canyon, in the whispers of the rock and the rustling of the trees beyond.

"There you are!" Your eldest nibling, now nearly as tall as you, spots you first. She shoves a mug in your hand and beckons you to a bench where your mutual friend is waving.

The wet fabric of your robes clings to your skin as you settle onto the wooden bench, the night air chilling the dampness further. Your friend grins at you, reaching over to poke at the moss-streaked hem of your sleeve. "Look at you! Again you come back from the wilds looking like you've been rolling in the dirt with the forest beasts."

You look at the soiled fabric and can't help but laugh. "Well at least the moss on these robes is lighter than their duties!"

Your friend chuckles and sips from their mug, shaking their head. "I still wonder why you keep going out there. The North Star is just a legend from long before our time."

"Maybe," you say, voice softer than before. "But stories don't listen, and they don't whisper things in the wind."

Your friend looks at you with a mixture of amusement and curiosity. "You're still out there listening, aren't you?"

She leans closer. "&hellip;&nbsp;Have you seen it?"

The question hangs between you for a beat, before your oldest cousin's horn blast cuts through the evening chatter. You turn your attention to the raised platform where she stands, her posture straight and confident in the ceremonial dress of the village's chief. The fringes of her hair are like a halo in the firelight. The villagers settle onto benches and blankets. Your spouse walks over and sits down beside you.

"Tonight, we celebrate!" Her voice carries across the clearing. "Our bountiful harvest, our growing children, our growing homes!" She gestures toward the table, the crowd, and the new houses now standing beyond the old village limits. "We celebrate the end of another season where we have fed and sheltered more of our people than the last!"

A murmur of agreement ripples through the crowd, tempered by the fact that this year's harvest is once again thinner than the last. Your spouse's hand rests lightly on your thigh. You don't join in the agreement, but you don't argue either.

"Our strength has brought us these blessings," your cousin continues, raising her arms. "With only our hands and our wills, we provide for ourselves. This solstice we prove our progress!"

The crowd erupts into applause, some standing, others stamping feet or banging wooden spoons against plates. The elders, like you, remain silent. Some close their eyes, too respectful to leave, but too ashamed to watch. Your fingers press into the wood grain as you watch your cousin stride to the edge of the platform, looking out at the gathering with a pleased smile. The firelight catches the pride in her eyes, the self-satisfaction in her stance.

You remember when she was smaller, when she would run ahead of you into the woods, laughing as she splashed through streams or climbed trees. She had loved the forest once, too.
Now, she speaks of it as something to be conquered.

Your spouse's hand moves to hold yours. "You know it's not that bad. We never had such feasts in the old days!" You turn your head to look at her, watching how the firelight plays across her face. Her smile is warm, genuine, and you can feel the sincerity in her words. She loves the village, loves the security of the homes that grow in strength and number every year. She loves that there is always enough food, always enough space. And she loves that it all means more time with you.

"I know," you respond quietly. "I'm... glad everyone has all this." You really do mean it. The new ways are bringing such happiness, your neighbors all seem less stressed and more lively living this way. You just can't help but think about what it means for their connections to the forest.

She squeezes your hand. "The forest will still be there tomorrow," she says, leaning her head against your shoulder briefly before turning back to the celebration.

Your oldest cousin speaks up again. "We now honor the old ways, which carried us far enough to build this village." She gestures toward you. "My cousin, who still walks the paths of the North Star."

You put on a smile and stand. The elders and your loved ones applaud you. The rest respond politely but restrained; you're a familiar curiosity to them, a symbol of a time they're steadily leaving behind.

The villagers shift their attention to you as you rise from the bench. Your cousin's smile widens slightly. You notice the calculated angle of her lips, the way she studies you for a moment before stepping aside to make way for you.

You say "My family, my neighbors. We have come so far, even since I was a child. Where we once foraged and gathered and hunted, we now sow and raise and harvest. Where we once took what the forest could provide and return what we did not use, we now keep all we raise and use it to raise and keep more."

A ripple of nodding spreads through the crowd. Your cousin's smile doesn't waver, but there's a sharpness in her eyes now—she recognizes the careful balance in your words, the way you're acknowledging both the new ways and the old without taking sides.

"My cousin speaks truly," she calls out before you can continue. "We have come so far, but the path forward is clear. We keep all we raise not from greed, but from wisdom. The forest cannot provide for our growing families forever. We must become masters of our own lands."
Some of the villagers cheer at this.

You nod respectfully at her and turn back to the crowd. "You know me for my journeys through the forest. I talk with the spirits and listen to the forest. Today, as with every solstice, I've heard the voices of our ancestors both human and not." A hush falls over the gathering. Even your cousin's supporters still themselves to listen. This is the part that makes them uneasy—the part where you speak for things they cannot hear.

"The North Star does not speak in human tongues," you continue, steady and measured. "But it watches. And I heard it whisper: that which is taken must be returned, in kind or in spirit. Our fields and livestock come from the plants and the animals we took and bred and raised. We take the land's strength to feed ourselves and our children, to grow our homes and our strength."

You place the basket on the ground. You remove fruits, mushrooms, meats, even pottery, showing each to the people as you talk. "The forest does not judge. It is our parent, and only wants to see us prosper. As we grow and leave our parent's nest, we must honor the kind and thorough upbringing it gave us. Our North Star guided us here." You look to the treeline for a moment and smile, remembering the lessons of the North Star. "Tonight, as we celebrate our growth, we also show our gratitude and respect to the forest which raised us. We offer this portion of what we have made for ourselves, giving it to the forest as a gesture of respect for its kindness in raising us so well."

The crowd watches in silence as you place each offering back into the basket. Fruits harvested from the orchards, meat from the cattle they've raised, even small clay pots made by the village potter. In the times of your parents, such bounty would have been luxurious. These are creations of the new ways, which came from the old.

"The North Star knows," you continue, "that we have changed. It sees how our lives have grown more comfortable, more certain. It understands and smiles upon us. But it also remembers how we used to live. It remembers the days when our gathering would spill twigs and rotten fruits, when our hunting would leave bones for the scavengers to pick."

"Now we keep the twigs and rot and create new soil with them, and use that soil in our fields. We clean and carve the bones to make these beautiful tools and decorations. These are very good, but give nothing to the forest." You gesture to the edge of the forest, and to the soil that hosts this whole ceremony, both drier than it was when you were a child. "To continue to provide for ourselves and to keep the village strong and healthy and respectful, we must give back to the forest."

"My neighbors and family, we now show our respect to the forest which raised us, by offering these a small sample of our new creations to the forest. Let us give a portion of every harvest, every meal, every pot, every blade we make." You pick up the basket. "Let this be the first of many such offerings! For our prosperity, our strength, our health, and our respect!" A weighted pause hangs in the air as you hold the basket aloft. Then, slowly, the crowd joins in a rolling cheer. The younger villagers clap readily, their faces open and eager. Your cousin smiles again—this time with something that looks almost like relief. The elders nod solemnly, their faces showing approval.

When the cheering dies down, you turn and carry the first offering toward the treeline. Some of the crowd stands to follow you, but keep a respectful distance. You feel the firelight dim as you step into the deeper shadow beneath the trees, where the moon's silvery light glows like a thousand tiny fires in the branches above.

You kneel at the base of a great oak, your old friend. "To the great oak, I give the mushrooms and fruits from our fields." You set the basket down and carefully remove the mushrooms and berries, placing them in a small hollow where the roots of the oak emerge from the soil. The moment your fingers touch the dirt, you feel it—an approving pulse, like a heartbeat in the earth itself. The moss beneath your knees stirs slightly, as if acknowledging your offering.

You stand again and walk to a clearing nearby the village. "To the grasses, I give the meats from our stock." You kneel again, carefully selecting the portions of meat—the choicest cuts, the scraps that would go to the dogs, the organs that would be discarded. You lay them on the ground where the grass parts naturally.

The grass sways in the evening breeze, but beneath your fingers as you place the last morsel, the motion changes. The blades curl inward, as if closing over the offering. A rush of approval washes through you, not in words, but in a feeling of completeness, as if something long left undone has finally been done.

You stand with the remaining offerings, the clay pots and trinkets, and move deeper into the forest, to the river where the village gathers water. "And to the river, I give that which we have created from clay and bone." You place the clay pots and bone tools carefully on a flat riverside stone. As soon as the last piece touches the stone, the water ripples outward in concentric circles, though no wind stirs the surface. The water itself feels warmer against your fingers as you dip them into the flow as it surges up the bank slightly, wetting the stone beneath your knees. A feeling of connection pulses between you and the river, an understanding, a new promise renewing an old love.

You turn back to the village, the homes bathed in golden firelight, with silhouettes of the trees and the villagers who followed you. You emerge from the trees, your cousin standing near the feast table with her hands clasped before her.

"The North Star accepts our offering," you announce, loud enough for all to hear. "It speaks of balance and plenty."
Your cousin steps forward and embraces you briefly. "Your wisdom brings peace to the village," she murmurs before raising her voice. "Let us feast! Let us celebrate our growth and our connection to the forest!"

You wake up and put on your robes. Your spouce helps you place your greying hair in a bun, and sees you off with a kiss and wellwishes.

As you pass the homes near your own, you admire the plants your neighbors have kept near their own doors. Bright flowers, wide-leafed vines, and verdent shrubs, all well-loved and curated in their garden pots. The people of the village have taken a love of nature within the village walls.

The ancient oak you bonded with as a child stands proudly and safely within the village walls now, the soil around it harder and drier these days.

The fields are more varied now, though their yields have declined back to the amounts that had the village concerned when you made that speech at a bonfire so many years ago. Still, you watch as the farmers faithfully carry offerings from their fields as they harvest, though their walk to the treeline is farther these days.

The enclave still sits near the village center. You step through the dusty curtain to see the other two elders settling at the table. Hearthlight casts long shadows across the three empty places at the table. You sit at the same one as always.

The elder with braided hair clears her throat. "The offerings seem to not be enough anymore," she says solemnly.

The elder with green eyes nods in agreement, tracing the grain of the table and watching the dust swirl in her wake. "If this continues, we risk the crops failing next year."

The table catches your arms after a deep breath. "My heart is warmed seeing everyone embrace this blend of new and old ways for so many years. The offerings worked well for the village that started them, but with the growth we've seen since then, this might as well be a different village entirely."

The elder with the braided hair leans forward, her eyes intent. "Perhaps it is time for us to revisit the tradition of the offerings," she suggests thoughtfully. "Not to abandon them, but to adapt them to our current circumstances."

"You may be onto something," the green-eyed elder muses. "We might increase the scope and scale of the offerings. Not just every harvest, but perhaps every moon?"

The elder with the braided hair leans back in her chair, a thoughtful expression on her face. "I think that could work," she says after a moment's consideration. "But we would need to be sure that the people understand why this change is necessary. We could hold a gathering, perhaps in the square near the great oak," she suggests. "We could explain the situation, show them how the soil in the forest is more fertile, how our dry soil risks next year's harvest."

You want to rant about how focusing on the harvest alone is undercutting the issue, that one must live one's life in harmony with the forest because that's where you come from, and to separate completely means... but you bite your tongue. You know these trusted two already understand and agree with you.

The elder with braided hair turns to you, a question in her eyes as she senses your reluctance to speak. "You are quiet, elder with robes of moss," she says gently. "What thoughts do you hold on this matter?"

"I isn't sustainable," you blurt out. With their attention, you continue. "We can ask them to offer more and more often, but that just passes the problem down another several solstices and we're right back here again!"

They listen with sympathetic eyes.

You let out a deep and pained sigh. "Forgive me," you begin slowly. "I know we must focus on sustaining our harvests, but..." You lean forward, your elbows resting on the table as you choose your next words deliberately.
You gesture vaguely toward the outer wall of the village.

The green-eyed elder exhales softly. "But the roots run deeper than that," she says, voice measured but laden with near resignation.

The elder with braided hair nods in agreement, though her expression remains gentle. "You are right... we cannot ignore the deeper truth causing the harvest concerns."

You rub your tired eyes, feeling the weight of years pressing down on your shoulders. You understand why the other two have looked so forlorn since your adolescence. "It's not just the harvest," you repeat, leaning back. "It's _what we've become_."

The green-eyed elder nods gravely, her fingers tracing patterns in the dust on the table and watching the dust curl in her wake. "The forest was home."

The elder with braided hair sighs, her lined face troubled. "We used to live as the trees live, not beside them but within them," she murmurs. "Now we sit here in our stone house, talking about the forest as if it is something other than us."

The three of you share a moment of profound silence, heavy with the absence of her name.

The green-eyed elder glances toward the curtained doorway, as if expecting her to stride in at any moment. The elder with braided hair simply watches you, waiting for you to acknowledge the obvious.

Your stand and walk to the hearth, lifting the kettle and pouring its water into a clay mug. The other elders watch as you select dried leaves from the mantle and place them in the water. You sit and take a long moment before sipping the tea.

The many energies of the forest run up your arms, through fingers buried in the soil. This far from the village, there are no human sounds. Only the leaves in the wind, birds and small mammals chirping and hooting, the rustle of the floor as critters scurry about...

and all of it suddenly seems to harmonize. The rustling, the chirping, everything in concert as you meditate with it all.

The concert becomes a slow, rhythmic beat, almost like a heartbeat. Almost like footsteps. Almost like it's saying "hello again."

Your eyes shoot open. Standing just in front of you is the mushroom-adorned dire wolf, wings wide and majestic as its antlers. The wolf tilts its head slightly, glowing eyes gleaming in the dappled light filtering through the canopy above. It moves forward with careful steps, each paw sinking into the detritus, leaving faint depressions that disappear just as quickly as they form.
 

Just about soon as you breathlessly and reverently mutter "North Star...", you jump to hug it around the neck. The great wolf lets out a resonant, contented huff. Mushroom caps brush your cheek as you nuzzle into its thick fur.

Its wings wrap around you in kind. "Hello again, my child."

##### death of the child

You return to the forest again, as you have every solstice morning. You go to the sacred canyon. This time, though, there is no one there to hear the wind tell your story.

#### SUMMARY

Many years later.

You grew up, did your best. You never forgot the forest, you returned to the sacred canyon annually, advocated in the village to pivot away from the new ways and back to the ways of the elders.

Eventually you become an elder, eventually the only one who advocates for harmony with the forest, the consequences of village expansion. Eventually the village center's soil becomes more dusty and barren as the village expands. This starts affecting the crops and livestock.

Some heed your words and start offering samples of their harvest to the forest, in hopes North Star will keep the village tied to the forest, and the treeline safe and healthy.

North Star eventually returns to work with you to set up a pact with the village, where y'all bring it offerings and it spends extra effort maintaining the forest around your village. Addicted, your people eventually ask it to also help their crops and livestock to be healthier and more bountiful, and it initially rejects this but eventually begrudgingly agrees in exchange for a blood sacrifice deep in the forest.

Your peers become addicted to this way of life, even setting up a temple for it, with a fancy altar for the offerings. You kinda find yourself lost in the rituals of it all, too.

This continued to endear North Star to the humans and your village, even after your death.
