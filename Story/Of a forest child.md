#### Characters

- **You** – An elder of the village. The child from chapter 1, deeply connected with nature thanks to her childhood encounter with the North Star
- **the North Star** – The god of the forest
- **your oldest cousin** – The chief of the village. She got her position through actions which oppose the forest but are so clearly beneficial to the village that the villagers love her en masse.



#### SUMMARY

Many years later.

You grew up, did your best. You never forgot the forest, you returned to the sacred canyon annually, advocated in the village to pivot away from the new ways and back to the ways of the elders.

Eventually you become an elder, eventually the only one who advocates for harmony with the forest, the consequences of village expansion. Eventually the village center's soil becomes more dusty and barren as the village expands. This starts affecting the crops and livestock.

Some heed your words and start offering samples of their harvest to the forest, in hopes North Star will keep the village tied to the forest, and the treeline safe and healthy.

North Star eventually returns to work with you to set up a pact with the village, where y'all bring it offerings and it spends extra effort maintaining the forest around your village. Addicted, your people eventually ask it to also help their crops and livestock to be healthier and more bountiful, and it initially rejects this but eventually begrudgingly agrees in exchange for a blood sacrifice deep in the forest.

Your peers become addicted to this way of life, even setting up a temple for it, with a fancy altar for the offerings. You kinda find yourself lost in the rituals of it all, too.

This continued to endear North Star to the humans and your village, even after your death.

the rhythm of the village draws you back. The quarry is louder now; you can hear the rhythmic strike of hammers, the scrape of stone. Your oldest cousin's face flashes before you: purposeful, intelligent, determined. You know she wants what's best for her people.

> You greet her, and offer your oldest cousin a berry.

She glances up from the fresh stack of stonework, sunlight glinting off her bronze hair as she wipes the dust from her forehead. The berry looks small against her large hands; she turns it gently, inspecting the glossy surface before popping it into her mouth. You watch the motion, how she bites down—her eyes widening, then narrowing slightly as she chews.

"Still grows near the river then," she says, brushing a stray crumb from her mouth.

You nod. "The river hasn't changed."

She gives a short, dry laugh that sounds more like breath than humor. "But we have."

You look toward the quarry and then back to her. She's clearly very proud. Her stance has that sureness of someone who's built something sturdy and knows it'll last. But you see also that she hasn't eaten berries in a long time. Her teeth sink into the fruit too sharply, like she's forgotten how to take a bite gently. She chews with the same rhythm as the stones being shaped behind her—methodical, certain.

"It tastes sweet," she says, lowering her voice. "But there's a bitterness too."

You notice her eyes, how they track past you toward the forest line. Her expression flickers from satisfaction to some thought you can't read.

"That's what I love about them!", you say. "To enjoy life, we must sample some of everything it has to offer. Living a life of grain and meat and beans is wonderful, but without something different to compare it to, we may never appreciate it for what it is. These wild berries offer both sweetness and bitterness, contrasting themselves, and in so we appreciate all they have to offer.

Your oldest cousin's hand stills, holding the stem of the berry. She turns it slowly, as if weighing some invisible thing. The rhythmic thud of the quarry continues behind her, steady as a heartbeat.

"That's true," she says finally. She looks down at the berry between her fingers, then at you. "But there's no need to risk poison just to know what sweetness is."

You don't answer right away. Your oldest cousin has never been one to see risk as part of growth. To her, growth is planned, deliberate—like the lines of the wall or the rows of her fields. She measures progress in stone and grain.

---


But her walls don't only keep danger out. They keep the forest out. The deer, the wolves, the bear that once wandered down to drink near the storehouses, all gone. The people stopped looking to the river. They look at their crops now. They look at the wall.

---

"I remember when the floods came," she says finally, still turning the pebble. "We lost three houses that season. Two of my friends' parents died." You do remember. The rain had come down in sheets for weeks. The village had smelled of wet clay and grief.

"That doesn't mean—" you start, but she's already shaking her head.

"I've never stopped moving since," she says. "And neither has this village. We build. We fix. We don't let things fall apart on our watch."

## Final scene

### Saying goodbye

<!-- The forest child walks off and dies. -->

The farmers stand from the altar at the center of the plaza, concluding their ritual. You bid them farewell. Even though the goat was too sickly to live, it still hurts to sacrifice her. The many years of milk and fur were well appreciated, as anyone can see by the lovingly crafted & sealed milk bottle and robe which lay on the sides of the altar.

The plaza has become something of a little forest in the village center. Partially your fault, you smile to yourself. You know the village loves it, even when they don't say it. You look around and see so many families enjoying its shade, its soft soil, all of its comfort, as they have for years now. The old elder house is a now a bakery. It seemed to have been very important to the new chief to start with that as her first project. You couldn't agree more.

With a firm grip on your staff, you make your way out of the plaza, walking past your house. Everything inside it seems to be as it's always been, just without your spouse there to keep care of the house and you, as she had to the very end.

The treeline is more lush than you've ever seen it. The forest has been able to grow more healthy here than ever, thanks to the plaza. You smile knowingly.

You return to the deep forest again, as you have every solstice morning. You go to the sacred canyon, the same place the North Star taught you how to listen to the wind carry stories of forest souls long gone. This time, though, no one is there to hear the wind tell your story.
